SYSTEMIC PROBLEMS IN DISTRIBUTED, AUTOMATED, AND AI-AUGMENTED SYSTEMS
===================================================================

This document lists the major structural problems that arise in distributed,
automated, or AI-augmented systems. Each problem is assigned an importance
value between 0 and 100, representing its systemic risk and impact at scale.

The problems are technology-agnostic and concern system structure, governance,
and long-term operability.


1. GOVERNANCE OF EXECUTION
-------------------------
Importance: 100 / 100

Definition:
Inability to deterministically prove what was executed, with which outcome,
under which authority, and with which final state.

Consequences:
- Actions cannot be audited or proven
- Legal and compliance exposure
- Impossible post-mortem analysis

Notes:
This is the foundational problem.
It is fully addressed by BISS-RIG and must not be weakened.


2. LOSS OF EXPLICIT CAUSALITY
----------------------------
Importance: 90 / 100

Definition:
The system cannot explain why an action occurred or what triggered it,
especially in concurrent or distributed environments.

Consequences:
- Root-cause analysis becomes speculative
- Debugging relies on guesswork
- Fragile behavior under concurrency

Notes:
This concerns ordering, triggering, and dependency chains.
Execution contracts alone do not solve this.


3. IMPLICIT STATE AND HIDDEN SIDE EFFECTS
----------------------------------------
Importance: 85 / 100

Definition:
The system reads or mutates state that is not explicitly declared or governed.

Consequences:
- Hidden coupling between components
- Non-local and cascading failures
- Unpredictable behavior

Notes:
Common with shared databases, caches, and global context.
Breaks reasoning and composability.


4. UNGOVERNED REASONING (AI / HEURISTICS)
----------------------------------------
Importance: 85 / 100

Definition:
Decisions are influenced by reasoning artifacts (models, prompts, rules)
that are not versioned, reproducible, or reviewable.

Consequences:
- Non-reproducible outcomes
- Drift without accountability
- Impossible rollback or comparison

Notes:
Critical in AI-augmented systems.
Reasoning must be treated as a governed artifact.


5. COMPLIANCE WITHOUT QUALITY
-----------------------------
Importance: 80 / 100

Definition:
The system remains formally correct while becoming functionally bad.

Consequences:
- Silent degradation
- Operational inefficiency
- Long-term value erosion

Notes:
Quality is outside execution correctness.
Requires separate measurement and monitoring.


6. ABSENCE OF SYSTEMIC VISIBILITY
--------------------------------
Importance: 75 / 100

Definition:
The system is understandable locally but opaque globally.

Consequences:
- Human operators overwhelmed
- Inability to assess global impact
- Strategic blindness

Notes:
Emerges from scale, not from bugs.
Requires synthesis, not more logs.


7. LACK OF GLOBAL ARBITRATION
----------------------------
Importance: 70 / 100

Definition:
The system has no mechanism to decide what is better when multiple valid
outcomes or objectives conflict.

Consequences:
- Conflicting local optimizations
- Suboptimal global behavior
- Organizational deadlocks

Notes:
Optimization and arbitration are not execution concerns.
They must remain separate from action.


8. SCALABILITY OF HUMAN SUPERVISION
----------------------------------
Importance: 65 / 100

Definition:
Even when everything is observable, humans cannot keep up with the volume
and complexity of signals.

Consequences:
- Alert fatigue
- Ignored warnings
- Loss of effective control

Notes:
This is a synthesis and tooling problem.
More data makes it worse, not better.


CONSOLIDATED IMPORTANCE SUMMARY
-------------------------------
- Governance of execution:        100
- Explicit causality:              90
- Implicit state:                 85
- Ungoverned reasoning (AI):       85
- Compliance without quality:      80
- Systemic visibility:             75
- Global arbitration:              70
- Human supervision scalability:   65


FINAL NOTE
----------
These are structural problems, not implementation defects.

NOTE: This list was written before UCC-SCOPE.txt was introduced. UCC-SCOPE.txt (Tier 1) now addresses Problems 2, 4, 5, 6, and 7 through the five-scope taxonomy and cross-scope causality rules. See SYSTEMIC-GOVERNANCE-SOLUTIONS.txt for the full resolution of all eight problems.
